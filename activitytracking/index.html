Activity Tracking

Goals:

Create an activity tracking component that can be reused throughout the app for seeing change history


My Role:

Requirements writing, research, wireframes

Collecting insight and business requirements

During the Rediker Software's initiative to replace the legacy desktop application with a web version, I was given a project to create an improved activity tracking feature. An important function in the Rediker AdminPlus legacy application was the ability for School Administrators to see a history of changes on stored field data for legal or referential purposes. I gained insight into the needs of this feature by meeting with stakeholders and writing notes on what was currently known about the functionality that exists in the legacy app. Turns out, the "History" feature was very convoluted and hidden under many unnecessary layers and needed improvement for the web. Working with stakeholders and the Project Manager, I wrote a list of business requirements that were refined over time as we reviewed it with others and learned more about the needs of this feature. One example of an added requirement was the ability to filter the results of line item changes, as the feature would often contain hundreds of results that needed to be looked through. Initial concepts, inspiration, and wireframes

As a starting point, I wanted to look at competing products that had this feature, and other existing change history components in general. I found some applications that had just one page of "History" that contained every change made within the product. Being that our product contained many different areas it would be implemented, and many different types of content (everything from birth dates to long paragraphs of notes), I made the decision early on to contain the activity tracking component within the page or tab it was relevant to. This would prevent users having to search through large lists of results, and provides a more focused experience. My biggest goal was to create a component that would anticipate different types of data and displays that could be quickly filtered and legible. Moving forward with priorities

As projects sometimes do, this one unfortunately got frozen for the time being. Shifting priorities pushed this feature back to our Beta release, rather than MVP, so I put the brakes on it right before I began to build a prototype. My plan was to make my wireframes lightly interactive so that I could share with our users and get some insight before moving onto visual design. I made sure to document what I had learned so far during my design sprints, so that I could pick it back up easily at a later time. The knowledge I gained from this project certainly translated to others I was working on in tandem, so in the end it had a positive ripple affect, and we would revisit activity tracking in later sprints.
